# Genetic Terminology Glossary (GLOSSARY.md)

## A

**Allele**: One of two or more alternative forms of a gene that arise by mutation and are found at the same place on a chromosome.

**Aneuploidy**: An abnormal number of chromosomes in a cell, either extra or missing.

**Anticipation**: The phenomenon where the symptoms of a genetic disorder become more severe or appear at an earlier age as the disorder is passed from one generation to the next.

**Autosomal**: Referring to any chromosome other than the sex chromosomes (X and Y).

## B

**Base Pair (bp)**: Two complementary nucleotides (A-T or G-C) that form the double helix of DNA.

**Bioinformatics**: The science of storing, retrieving, and analyzing biological data, especially genetic sequences.

**Biallelic**: Having two different alleles at a given locus on homologous chromosomes.

## C

**Carrier**: An individual who carries one copy of a recessive gene for a genetic condition but does not show symptoms.

**Chromosome**: A structure containing genetic material (DNA) found in the nucleus of cells.

**Codon**: A sequence of three nucleotides that corresponds to a specific amino acid or stop signal during protein synthesis.

**Copy Number Variation (CNV)**: A type of structural variation in which segments of the genome are duplicated or deleted.

**CRISPR**: Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats; a family of DNA sequences found in prokaryotic genomes that are derived from virus fragments.

## D

**De novo**: Newly occurring in a family, not inherited from parents.

**Dominant**: An inheritance pattern where one copy of an altered gene is sufficient to cause the condition.

**Dosage Compensation**: Mechanisms to equalize expression of genes on sex chromosomes between males and females.

**Down Syndrome**: A genetic disorder caused by the presence of all or part of a third copy of chromosome 21.

## E

**Epigenetics**: Changes in gene expression that do not involve alterations to the underlying DNA sequence.

**Exome**: The portion of the genome that contains exons, which encode proteins.

**Exon**: A coding sequence of a gene that is expressed as a protein.

## F

**Frameshift Mutation**: A genetic mutation caused by insertion or deletion of nucleotides that shifts the reading frame of the gene.

**Founder Effect**: The reduced genetic diversity that results when a population is descended from a small number of colonizing ancestors.

## G

**Gamete**: A mature sexual reproductive cell (sperm or egg) that unites with another cell to form a zygote.

**Gene**: A segment of DNA that contains instructions for making a specific protein or RNA molecule.

**Gene Expression**: The process by which information from a gene is used to create a functioning gene product, usually a protein.

**Gene Therapy**: The introduction of normal genes into cells to replace defective genes.

**Genome**: The complete set of genetic material in an organism.

**Genotype**: The genetic constitution of an individual organism.

**Germline**: Refers to gametes and the cell lineage that gives rise to gametes.

## H

**Haplotype**: A group of genes or DNA sequences that tend to be inherited together.

**Heterozygous**: Having two different alleles at a given locus on homologous chromosomes.

**Homozygous**: Having identical alleles at a given locus on homologous chromosomes.

**Horizontal Gene Transfer**: The transfer of genetic material between organisms that are not parent and offspring.

## I

**In silico**: Performed on a computer or via computer simulation.

**In situ**: In the original position; referring to performing experiments or observations in the original location.

**Inheritance Pattern**: The manner in which a genetic trait or condition is passed from one generation to the next.

**Insertion**: A type of mutation involving the addition of one or more nucleotides into a DNA sequence.

**Intervening Sequence (Intron)**: A non-coding sequence of DNA that interrupts a coding sequence (exon).

## J

**Jumping Gene (Transposon)**: A DNA sequence that can change its position within the genome.

## K

**Knockout**: A genetic technique in which a gene is inactivated to study its function.

## L

**Linkage**: The tendency for genes located close together on a chromosome to be inherited together.

**Linkage Disequilibrium**: A non-random association of alleles at different loci in a population.

**LOD Score**: Logarithm of odds score used in genetic linkage analysis.

## M

**Marker**: A gene or DNA sequence with a known location on a chromosome that can be used to identify individuals or species.

**Mendelian**: Referring to the laws of inheritance described by Gregor Mendel.

**Metagenomics**: The study of genetic material recovered directly from environmental samples.

**Missense Mutation**: A point mutation that results in a codon that codes for a different amino acid.

**Mitochondria**: Cellular organelles that produce energy through cellular respiration and contain their own DNA.

**Modifier Gene**: A gene that affects the expression of another gene.

**Multifactorial**: Influenced by multiple genetic and environmental factors.

## N

**Nonsense Mutation**: A point mutation that results in a premature stop codon.

**Nucleotide**: The basic building block of DNA or RNA, consisting of a nitrogen-containing base, a phosphate group, and a sugar molecule.

## O

**Oligogenic**: Controlled by a small number of genes, usually fewer than five.

**Oncogene**: A gene that when activated can cause a normal cell to become cancerous.

## P

**PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)**: A technique used to amplify specific DNA sequences in vitro.

**Pedigree**: A chart showing the occurrence and appearance of phenotypes of a particular gene or organism and its ancestors from one generation to the next.

**Penetrance**: The proportion of individuals carrying a particular variant who also express an associated trait.

**Phenotype**: The observable physical or biochemical characteristics of an organism, determined by both genetic makeup and environmental influences.

**Polymorphism**: The occurrence of different alleles at a genetic locus with appreciable frequency in a population.

**Promoter**: A region of DNA where transcription of a gene begins.

## Q

**Quantitative Trait Locus (QTL)**: A region of DNA that is associated with a particular phenotypic trait which varies continuously.

## R

**Recessive**: An inheritance pattern where two copies of an altered gene are needed to cause the condition.

**Recombination**: The process by which genetic material is exchanged between chromosomes during meiosis.

**Replication**: The process by which DNA makes a copy of itself.

**Reporter Gene**: A gene used to determine whether a particular DNA construct has been successfully inserted into cells.

## S

**Sequencing**: Determining the order of nucleotides in a DNA or RNA molecule.

**Sex-linked**: Referring to genes located on sex chromosomes (X or Y).

**Silencer**: A DNA sequence that causes a gene to be turned off or expressed at a lower level.

**Single Nucleotide Polymorphism (SNP)**: A variation in a single nucleotide that occurs at a specific position in the genome.

**Southern Blot**: A method used to detect specific DNA sequences in DNA samples.

**Spliceosome**: A large ribonucleoprotein complex that removes introns from precursor mRNA.

**Stop Codon**: A sequence of three nucleotides in mRNA that signals the termination of protein synthesis.

## T

**Telomere**: The protective structure at the end of a chromosome.

**Terminator**: A sequence of DNA that marks the end of a gene and signals for transcription to stop.

**Trait**: A genetically determined characteristic.

**Transcription**: The process of copying a segment of DNA into RNA.

**Transduction**: The transfer of bacterial DNA from one bacterium to another by a virus.

**Transfection**: The process of deliberately introducing foreign DNA, RNA, or protein into eukaryotic cells.

**Transfer RNA (tRNA)**: A type of RNA that carries amino acids to the ribosome during protein synthesis.

**Translation**: The process of producing proteins based on the sequence of messenger RNA.

**Trisomy**: The presence of three copies of a chromosome instead of the usual two.

## U

**Upregulation**: An increase in the expression of a gene or the activity of a protein at the post-transcriptional level.

## V

**Variant**: A change in the DNA sequence compared to a reference sequence.

**Vector**: A vehicle used to transfer genetic material into a target cell.

**Vertical Transmission**: The passage of genetic material from parent to offspring.

## W

**Whole Exome Sequencing (WES)**: A technique that sequences only the protein-coding regions of genes.

**Whole Genome Sequencing (WGS)**: A process that determines the complete DNA sequence of an organism's genome.

## X

**X-Chromosome Inactivation**: The process by which one of the two X chromosomes in female mammals is inactivated.

## Y

**Y-Chromosome**: The male sex chromosome that determines male sex characteristics.

## Z

**Zygosity**: The degree of similarity between the alleles of a diploid organism at a given genetic locus.

**Zygote**: A cell formed by the union of two gametes (fertilized ovum).

## Statistical Terms

**Effect Size**: A quantitative measure of the magnitude of the difference between two groups.

**Odds Ratio (OR)**: A measure of association between an exposure and an outcome.

**P-value**: The probability of obtaining test results at least as extreme as the results actually observed during the test.

**Confidence Interval**: A type of estimate computed from the statistics of the observed data, giving a range of values for an unknown parameter.

**Power**: The probability that a statistical test will detect an effect that is present.

**Cohort**: A group of subjects who share a defining characteristic and who are followed up over time.

**Case-Control Study**: An observational study in which two existing groups differing in outcome are identified and compared on the basis of some supposed causal attribute.

**Meta-Analysis**: A statistical analysis that combines the results of multiple scientific studies.
